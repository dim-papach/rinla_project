Fix your FITS (fyf)

Resolving satellite, cosmic rays and bad pixels in astronomical images

Papachristopoulos Dimitrios¹, Akras Stavros²
¹University Name, ²Institution Name,

Abstract

Meteors, satellites, cosmic rays, and bad pixels are just a few of the obstacles that create artifacts in images and complicate the interpretation of astronomical observations. Satellites are a very common problem nowadays due to the launch of thousands of them by various companies and nations around the world. With limited time and resources, every astronomical image holds significant value, and often astronomers must work with compromised images. By employing the Integrated Nested Laplace Approximation (INLA) algorithm, which offers a quicker alternative to the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, we have created an open-source command-line interface (CLI) tool that can restore images. The algorithm has been applied to the planetary nebulae NGC 7009 and the HII region M16  with an accuracy between 70-99% (Structural similarity index measure; SSIM) and a residual percentage between -8% and 8% with a mean residual close to 0.

Aims

We created the FYF command-line interface (CLI) tool to process astronomical FITS images. The tool is designed to handle various artifacts such as:

  • cosmic rays,
  • satellite trails,
  • bad pixels,
  • astronomical objects, such as Low Ionization Structures (LIS)

    • using the R-INLA algorithm.

    R-INLA

    Integrated Nested Laplace Approximation (INLA) algorithm is a potentially quicker alternative to the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method, which is commonly used for Bayesian inference for latent Gaussian models. INLA is particularly useful for spatial and temporal data analysis, making it suitable for processing astronomical images. The algorithm works by approximating the posterior distribution of the latent variables in a Gaussian Markov random field (GMRF) framework, meaning that each pixel in the image is treated not as an independent entity but rather is influenced by its neighboring pixels. By applying INLA, the algorithm treats the corrupted image as a latent field, which we want to recover. The output of the INLA algorithm is a filled image where the missing or corrupted pixels are estimated based on the surrounding pixel values.

    FYF Command-Line Usage

    $ fyf --input input_file.fits --output output_file.fits
